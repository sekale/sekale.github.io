Minimalist Design Principles

Bland is the new brand!


A few key principles of good design (also plays into minimalistic approach):

  • Form follows function – No matter how sexy your product looks, if it does nothing, it will still be a weak product. Good visual design can only make your product go from good to great!
  • Relative contrast: Highlight the most important thing on your app but only do so considering relative contrast in mind. For instance, you could have your main content as relatively low contrast if it will be encountered in the workflow. A top-down approach to contrast seems to be key for maintaining a neat storyline.
  • Colors: As a rule of thumb, don’t cause the “rainbow” effect. Use a few colors at the most to highlight actionable things in a UI as compared to the 1995 Microsoft website design below.
  • Consistency >> Complexity
  • Typography: Keep a few consistent fonts and stick to the style throughout
  • Separation (Containers & Dividers): When showing sections, make sure text color is the brightest element on the screen. The borders should be relatively low contrast so that the effect on text is enunciated.
  • Contextualize: Present items as a group of elements rather than disjointed sets if they are representing different values of the same root entity.
  • For instance, in a list, only put spaces between list items if the distinction between each list item is significant.

    A lot of my opinions about this content is somewhat derivative of this BB article I was attending a product design class, and someone pointed out that bland seems to be the default choice for colors and design. Bland = Do one thing well + under-advertise on your product Minimalistic design with neutral colors: Gone are the days where flashy colors on an HTML5 (with some cool JS animations) was the major seller. Neutral pastel colors now take precedence and noisy UIs are replaced with relevant contextualized blocks. In product they come off as the under-confident backbencher who challenges the status quo and makes big wins. I think the trend is mostly a net-positive but it exemplifies a growing trend of marketing a product as doing one thing but really well.
